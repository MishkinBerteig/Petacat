### Coderack (Stochastic Scheduler)

**Topic key:** `coderack`  
**Summary:** A stochastic scheduler that probabilistically selects from a pool of small specialized agents (codelets) to drive all processing.

The Coderack is Metacat's stochastic scheduler -- a pool of small, specialized agents called codelets that collectively drive all processing. There is no central controller in Metacat; instead, behavior emerges from the probabilistic selection and execution of many codelets, each performing a single micro-task. The Coderack can hold up to 100 codelets at any time, distributed across seven urgency levels: extremely-low, very-low, low, medium, high, very-high, and extremely-high.

Codelet selection is probabilistic and temperature-modulated. Each codelet's probability of being selected is proportional to its urgency level, but the degree to which urgency matters depends on the current temperature. At high temperature (the system is confused, little structure has been built), selection is nearly random -- all codelets have roughly equal chances regardless of urgency. At low temperature (strong structures exist, the system is confident), selection becomes greedy -- high-urgency codelets are strongly preferred. This temperature-regulated selection implements what Hofstadter calls a 'parallel terraced scan': not true parallelism, but a probabilistic approximation where many potential avenues are explored in proportion to their promise.

Metacat uses over 40 distinct codelet types, organized into several families. Scout codelets explore the Workspace for potential structures: bottom-up scouts notice salient objects, top-down scouts look for instances of highly-active Slipnet concepts, and thematic scouts pursue structures consistent with dominant Themespace patterns. Evaluator codelets assess proposed structures for strength and coherence. Builder codelets promote evaluated structures to built status, potentially fighting and breaking competing structures in the process. Breaker codelets remove weak structures. Rule codelets discover, evaluate, and translate transformation rules. Bridge codelets establish mappings between objects across strings. Progress-watcher codelets monitor whether the system is making forward progress. Jootser codelets detect and respond to repetitive failure patterns. Answer-justifier codelets validate proposed answers.

New codelets are posted to the Coderack during each update cycle (every 15 codelet executions). Bottom-up codelets are posted in proportion to object salience in the Workspace, ensuring that attention-worthy objects get explored. Top-down codelets are posted in proportion to Slipnet node activation, ensuring that currently-relevant concepts drive exploration. Thematic codelets are posted when Themespace patterns reach dominance, biasing the system toward the emerging interpretation. This continuous replenishment keeps the Coderack stocked with a mix of exploratory and focused agents.

The Coderack's stochastic nature is fundamental to Metacat's design philosophy. By avoiding deterministic control, the system can explore unexpected avenues and discover creative analogies that a rigid controller would miss. The randomness is not noise -- it is a principled search strategy that balances exploration and exploitation through temperature regulation.

**Related concepts:** `codelet`, `urgency`, `stochastic_selection`, `temperature`, `parallel_terraced_scan`, `scout`, `evaluator`, `builder`, `bin`

### Commentary

**Topic key:** `commentary`  
**Summary:** Natural-language narration of what Metacat is doing and why, generated as the run unfolds.

The Commentary panel shows a running description of Metacat's cognitive activity during a run. Commentary is generated from the conceptual representations that Metacat builds -- it is not natural-language understanding, but template-based transcription of Slipnet concepts, built structures, discovered rules, and self-watching events into English prose.

Commentary has two modes, controlled by the 'Eliza commentary' checkbox in the Run Controls Settings group:

- Eliza mode (default): Friendly, narrative-style descriptions. For example: 'I notice abc has a successor pattern, and I'm looking at xyz to see if it has the same.' This mode makes runs easier to follow for new users and presentations.

- Technical mode: Precise, formal descriptions that name the specific structures and slippages involved. For example: 'Built successor-group bond between letters at positions 0 and 1 of initial-string.' This mode is useful for debugging and for users familiar with Metacat's internal vocabulary.

The commentary accumulates as new events happen: bonds built, groups formed, bridges made, rules discovered, snags encountered, answers found. Together with the Temporal Trace, it is the main way users observe what the engine is doing at a high level without having to read every codelet execution.

**Related concepts:** `trace`, `eliza_mode`

### Episodic Memory

**Topic key:** `memory`  
**Summary:** A cross-run memory store that saves rich answer and snag descriptions, supporting reminding, comparison, and commentary across problem-solving episodes.

Episodic Memory is Metacat's persistent store of past problem-solving experiences, preserving rich descriptions of answers and snags across multiple runs. While the Workspace and Temporal Trace are reset at the beginning of each run, Episodic Memory accumulates over the system's lifetime, giving Metacat the ability to remember, compare, and learn from its previous analogies. This addresses one of the major weaknesses of Copycat, which treats every run as an isolated event with no memory of past results.

The primary unit stored in Episodic Memory is the answer description -- a structured characterization of a discovered answer that includes the problem strings, the answer string itself, the rules used to derive it (with abstractness ratings), the supporting bridges and concept-mappings, the Themespace activation pattern at the time of discovery, the temperature at which the answer was found, a quality score from 0 to 100, and any unjustified slippages (concept substitutions that were not supported by the built structures). Episodic Memory also stores snag descriptions that record failure episodes, capturing the themes and structures involved when the system got stuck.

Episodic Memory supports three key operations. Reminding occurs automatically when a new answer is found: the system compares the new answer's thematic profile against all stored answer descriptions, and any sufficiently similar past answers are reactivated, brought back to the system's attention. This is analogous to the human experience of being reminded of a past insight when encountering a similar situation. The similarity metric is based on shared themes rather than surface features of the strings, so the system can be reminded across different problems if the underlying conceptual patterns match.

Comparison allows two answers to be directly contrasted, analyzing their shared versus differing themes, rules, and supporting structures. This enables Metacat to articulate why one answer is better than another, or how two different answers to the same problem reflect different perceptual interpretations. The comparison mechanism works at the level of themes and concept-mappings, not raw strings, enabling deep structural comparison.

Commentary is the system's ability to generate natural-language English descriptions of answers and comparisons. This is not natural language understanding -- it is template-based transcription from the Slipnet concept vocabulary into English phrases. For example, Metacat can say 'The answer xyz was produced by replacing the rightmost letter by its successor' or 'Both answers share the theme of left-to-right directionality in their top bridges.' This commentary capability makes the system's reasoning accessible and interpretable.

**Related concepts:** `answer_description`, `snag_description`, `reminding`, `comparison`, `commentary`, `quality_score`, `thematic_profile`, `cross_run`

### Problem Input

**Topic key:** `problem_input`  
**Summary:** The four letter strings that define an analogy problem: Initial, Modified, Target, and (optionally) Answer.

The Problem Input panel is where you specify the analogy problem Metacat will attempt to solve. Every problem has the form 'Initial -> Modified; Target -> ?'. For example, 'abc -> abd; xyz -> ?' asks Metacat to find an analogous transformation for xyz given that abc became abd.

The four fields are:

- Initial: The starting string of the top row, e.g. 'abc'. This is what the transformation begins with.
- Modified: The result of the top-row transformation, e.g. 'abd'. Metacat will discover rules that describe how Initial became Modified.
- Target: The starting string of the bottom row, e.g. 'xyz'. Metacat will try to apply an analogous transformation to this string.
- Answer (optional): If you supply this, Metacat enters justification mode and tries to explain why this specific answer is (or isn't) a good analogy, rather than discovering an answer from scratch.

The Demo Problem dropdown lets you load pre-configured problems from the dissertation, including well-known examples like the 'abc -> abd; xyz -> ?' problem and many harder variations. Each demo also has a preset random seed for deterministic replay of known-good runs.

Problem Input fields are disabled while a run is in progress. To change the problem, first stop or reset the current run.

**Related concepts:** `workspace`, `justify_mode`, `deterministic_replay`

### Run Controls

The Run Controls panel has three grouped sections:

A polling interval of 0 means continuous updates (~100ms); dragging the slider right produces less frequent updates for lower UI overhead. - Breakpoint: Pause the run when the codelet count reaches a specific value. Useful for debugging or stepping through a specific point in a known run.

The status box at the bottom shows the current run number, total codelets executed so far, and the type of the most recently executed codelet.

### Slipnet (Semantic Network)

**Topic key:** `slipnet`  
**Summary:** A semantic network of 59 concept nodes whose activation levels enable fluid conceptual slippage during analogy-making.

The Slipnet is Metacat's long-term conceptual memory, implemented as a semantic network of 59 concept nodes connected by weighted links. Unlike the Workspace, which changes from run to run, the Slipnet's structure is fixed -- it represents the system's permanent knowledge about the letter-string domain. Nodes represent concepts such as individual letters (a through z), string positions (leftmost, rightmost, middle, whole, single), object categories (letter, group), bond types (sameness, successor, predecessor), directions (left, right), group categories (same-group, successor-group, predecessor-group), bond facets (letter-category, length, alphabetic-position, direction), and small numbers (one through five).

Each Slipnet node has an activation level ranging from 0 to 100. Activation flows into the Slipnet from the Workspace: whenever a built structure uses a concept, that concept's node receives activation. Once activated, nodes spread activation to their neighbors along weighted links, and activation decays over time. When a node's activation exceeds 50%, it is considered fully active and begins to influence processing top-down -- for example, by causing top-down scout codelets to be posted that look for instances of that concept in the Workspace.

The most important property of the Slipnet is that it enables conceptual slippage, the key mechanism underlying fluid analogy. Slippage occurs when activation patterns cause normally-distinct concepts to be treated as interchangeable. For example, when the concept 'opposite' becomes highly active (perhaps because the problem involves a reversal), it can cause 'leftmost' and 'rightmost' to slip into each other, or 'successor' and 'predecessor' to swap. This is not a logical deduction but an emergent perceptual shift driven by activation dynamics. The degree of slippage depends on link weights and current activation levels, making it context-sensitive.

The Slipnet has five types of links: category links (connecting instances to their category, e.g., 'a' to 'letter'), instance links (the reverse), property links (connecting concepts to their properties), lateral links (connecting related concepts at the same level), and lateral-sliplinks (connecting concepts that can slip into each other, such as 'left' and 'right' via 'opposite'). Each node also has a conceptual depth value -- an intrinsic property indicating how abstract or fundamental the concept is. Deep concepts like 'opposite' and 'successor' are harder to activate but, once active, exert stronger influence and resist decay more than shallow concepts like individual letters.

Slipnet nodes can be clamped -- held at full activation for a fixed period -- during initialization or during snag-response episodes. Clamping forces the system to attend to particular concepts, overriding the normal activation dynamics. This is used both at startup (to bootstrap relevant concepts) and during jootsing (to force exploration of alternative interpretations).

**Related concepts:** `activation`, `conceptual_depth`, `slippage`, `spreading_activation`, `clamping`, `link_type`, `concept_node`, `lateral_sliplink`, `fully_active`

### Temperature

**Topic key:** `temperature`  
**Summary:** A global control parameter (0-100) that serves as an inverse measure of problem organization and regulates the randomness of all stochastic decisions.

Temperature is a single global value ranging from 0 to 100 that serves as an inverse measure of how well Metacat has organized the current problem. It is computed from the quantity and quality of built structures in the Workspace and is updated every 15 codelet executions (each update cycle). Temperature is not merely a diagnostic -- it actively regulates the randomness of stochastic decisions throughout the system, functioning as the master control knob for the exploration-exploitation tradeoff.

At high temperature (near 100), little structure has been built and the system is in a state of high uncertainty. Codelet selection from the Coderack becomes nearly random, with urgency levels having little effect on selection probability. This allows broad, unfocused exploration of the problem space, giving weak or unlikely ideas a chance to be pursued. Structure-building decisions are also more random at high temperature -- the system is more willing to accept weak structures and less likely to break existing ones. High temperature characterizes the early stages of a run, before the system has formed any coherent interpretation.

At low temperature (near 0), strong structures have been built and the system is confident in its interpretation. Codelet selection becomes greedy, with high-urgency codelets strongly preferred over low-urgency ones. Structure-building is more discriminating -- only strong structures survive, and weak competitors are readily broken. Low temperature characterizes the later stages of a run, when the system is converging on an answer and refining its interpretation.

Temperature can be clamped -- held at a fixed value -- during snag-response periods. When a progress-watcher codelet detects that the system is stuck, it may clamp the temperature at a moderately high value to force the system out of a local minimum. By preventing temperature from dropping, clamping ensures that the system continues to explore rather than prematurely converging on an inadequate interpretation. The clamp is released after a fixed period, allowing temperature to resume its normal dynamics.

The temperature formula takes into account the total number of built structures, their average strength, and the coverage of the Workspace (how much of each string is accounted for by bonds, groups, and bridges). A Workspace with many strong structures covering all four strings will produce a very low temperature, while a Workspace with few or weak structures will produce a high temperature. This creates a natural feedback loop: as the system builds better structures, temperature drops, making the system more focused, which in turn helps it build even better structures -- until it converges on an answer or encounters a snag.

**Related concepts:** `exploration_exploitation`, `clamping`, `update_cycle`, `structure_strength`, `randomness`, `convergence`, `unhappiness`, `rule_factor`

### Themespace (Self-Watching)

**Topic key:** `themespace`  
**Summary:** The self-watching mechanism that tracks activated thematic patterns across conceptual dimensions, enabling Metacat to monitor and influence its own perception.

The Themespace is Metacat's primary innovation over its predecessor Copycat, implementing the self-watching capability that gives the system insight into its own cognitive processes. While Copycat perceives patterns in its input data, it has no awareness of patterns in its own processing. The Themespace closes this gap by tracking themes -- activated patterns along conceptual dimensions that characterize the dominant perceptual interpretation being built at any moment.

Themes are organized into clusters defined by two coordinates: a bridge type and a conceptual dimension. The three bridge types are top-bridge (mapping between initial and modified strings), bottom-bridge (mapping between target and answer strings), and vertical-bridge (mapping between initial and target, or modified and answer). Dimensions include direction, group-category, letter-category, bond-facet, string-position, and others. A theme within a cluster represents a specific concept-mapping along that dimension for that bridge type -- for example, 'top-bridge, direction: left-to-right identity' or 'vertical-bridge, letter-category: a-to-x slippage'.

Each theme has both positive (supporting) and negative (inhibiting) activation values. Themes interact within and across clusters through excitatory and inhibitory dynamics. Within a cluster, themes representing similar or compatible mappings excite each other, while themes representing opposing mappings inhibit each other. Across clusters, activation propagates when different bridge types share the same dimension and concept-mapping -- for example, if the top bridge maps direction left-to-right, this supports a similar mapping in the bottom bridge.

When a theme's activation exceeds all competitors in its cluster by a sufficient margin, it becomes dominant. Dominant themes exert thematic pressure on the rest of the system, influencing which codelets get posted to the Coderack and biasing structure evaluation. This feedback loop is the heart of Metacat's self-watching: the system observes its own emerging interpretation (via theme activation), and that observation feeds back into perception (via thematic pressure on codelet posting and structure evaluation). The result is a system that can recognize when it is converging on a particular interpretation and either reinforce that convergence or, when problems arise, deliberately shift away from it.

The Themespace also plays a critical role in answer characterization. When Metacat finds an answer, the current Themespace activation pattern provides a rich, multidimensional description of the interpretation that led to that answer. This thematic fingerprint is stored in Episodic Memory alongside the answer, enabling the system to compare answers not just by their surface form but by the deep perceptual patterns that produced them. Two different answers to the same problem will have different thematic profiles, allowing Metacat to explain why one interpretation is more compelling than another.

**Related concepts:** `theme`, `cluster`, `bridge_type`, `dimension`, `thematic_pressure`, `dominance`, `self_watching`, `concept_mapping`, `excitation`, `inhibition`

### Temporal Trace

**Topic key:** `trace`  
**Summary:** A chronological event log recording all significant cognitive events during a run, enabling retrospective analysis of the system's processing history.

The Temporal Trace is Metacat's chronological record of all significant cognitive events that occur during a run. Every time a bond is built or broken, a group is formed or dissolved, a bridge is established, a rule is discovered, a snag is encountered, a clamp period begins or ends, or an answer is found, the event is recorded in the Trace with a sequential event number, an event type, and references to the associated structures. The Trace provides Metacat with something Copycat entirely lacks: a memory of its own processing history.

The Trace serves as the raw data source for Metacat's self-watching mechanisms. Progress-watcher codelets periodically examine the Trace to determine whether the system is making forward progress. If a progress-watcher detects that the system has been running for many codelets without building any new significant structures, it records a snag event in the Trace and initiates a response. Jootser codelets go further, analyzing sequences of snag events to detect repetitive failure patterns -- cases where the system keeps trying and failing to build the same kind of structure.

The Trace also supports Metacat's ability to reason about its own behavior after the fact. When comparing two answers to the same problem, the system can examine their respective Traces to understand how the processing differed -- which structures were built first, where snags occurred, and how the system recovered. This temporal dimension adds depth to Metacat's self-understanding beyond what the Themespace alone provides.

Each event in the Trace captures not just what happened but when it happened relative to other events. This ordering is essential for detecting temporal patterns. For example, a jootser codelet might notice that the system has built and then broken the same type of bond three times in the last 50 codelet steps, indicating that it is stuck in a loop. Without the temporal ordering provided by the Trace, such patterns would be invisible.

The Trace is reset at the beginning of each run, so it captures the processing history of a single problem-solving episode. Cross-run memory is handled by Episodic Memory, which stores compressed answer and snag descriptions rather than raw event sequences.

**Related concepts:** `event`, `event_type`, `snag`, `progress_watcher`, `jootser`, `temporal_pattern`, `processing_history`, `clamp_period`

### Workspace

**Topic key:** `workspace`  
**Summary:** The central problem-solving space holding four letter strings and all perceptual structures built during a run.

The Workspace is the central arena where Metacat's analogy-making unfolds. It contains four letter strings arranged in two rows: the top row holds the initial string and its modified counterpart (representing the given transformation, such as 'abc' to 'abd'), and the bottom row holds the target string and the answer string (the analogy to be discovered, such as 'xyz' to '?'). Every perceptual structure that Metacat builds during a run lives in the Workspace, and the interplay among these structures determines the system's evolving interpretation of the problem.

The Workspace holds six types of perceptual structures. Letters are the atomic elements, each positioned within its parent string. Descriptions attach semantic properties to objects, such as letter-category, string-position, length, and direction. Bonds represent relationships between adjacent objects along a particular facet -- for example, a successor bond on the letter-category facet between 'a' and 'b'. Groups are structured collections of letters or subgroups that share a common bond pattern, direction, and category. Bridges map objects across different strings, carrying concept-mappings that describe how one object's descriptions correspond to another's -- via identity, slippage, or coattail mappings. Rules are transformation descriptions that characterize how one string changes into another, built from the bridges that connect them.

Every structure in the Workspace passes through a lifecycle of three proposal levels: proposed, evaluated, and built. A scout codelet first proposes the structure, then an evaluator codelet assesses its strength, and finally a builder codelet promotes it to built status if it is strong enough. If the structure is too weak, it fizzles and is discarded. Built structures can also be broken later if a competing structure proves stronger, keeping the Workspace interpretation fluid.

Structure strength is a crucial concept. Each structure's strength is computed from two components: its internal coherence (how well its parts fit together) and its external support (how well it fits with other structures already in the Workspace). These values are recomputed every update cycle and feed into temperature calculation. Objects in the Workspace also have importance and salience values that determine how likely they are to be noticed by scout codelets, creating a form of emergent attention.

The Workspace is not a passive container but an active participant in the architecture's control loop. During each update cycle, the system scans the Workspace to spread activation to the Slipnet and Themespace, recompute structure strengths, update object salience, and recalculate temperature. The state of the Workspace thus continuously shapes which codelets get posted and how the system explores the problem space.

**Related concepts:** `letter`, `string`, `bond`, `group`, `bridge`, `rule`, `description`, `proposal_level`, `strength`, `salience`

### Clear Episodic Memory

**Topic key:** `admin_clear_memory`  
**Summary:** Removes all stored answer and snag descriptions from past runs.

**What this does (user perspective):**

- Removes all stored answer descriptions and snag descriptions that Metacat has accumulated from past runs.

- Episodic Memory will begin accumulating new entries on subsequent runs as answers and snags are discovered.

### Full Reset (delete everything)

**Topic key:** `admin_full_reset`  
**Summary:** Completely resets Petacat -- deletes all runs, clears memory, and returns the UI to its initial state.

**What this does (user perspective):**

- Completely resets Petacat to its initial state, as if you had just opened it for the first time.
- Stops any currently running engine execution.
- Deletes EVERY run ever created, including all their step-by-step history, trace events, and cycle snapshots.
- Clears all episodic memory (answer and snag descriptions).
- Resets all UI panels to their empty/default state.
- This is the "start completely over" button. Use it when you want a clean slate with no leftover data from previous sessions.

**Technical details:**

- Calls DELETE /api/runs, which deletes all rows from the runs, cycle_snapshots, trace_events, and answer_descriptions database tables.
- Calls DELETE /api/memory to clear the in-memory episodic memory store.
- Resets all Zustand store state: runId, workspace, slipnet, coderack, themespace, trace, memory, temperature, commentary, and codeletCount.
- Bumps the store epoch to force all components to re-fetch and re-render.
- Sets the internal _stopRequested flag to halt any in-progress run loop.
- Database seed data (slipnet nodes, codelet types, engine parameters, urgency levels, formula coefficients, demo problems, etc.) is NOT affected. Only user-created run data is deleted.

## Glossary

Terms used throughout the architecture. Served by `GET /api/docs/glossary` and `GET /api/docs/glossary/{term}`.

### Activation

**Topic key:** `activation`  
**Summary:** The current activity level (0-100) of a Slipnet node or Themespace theme, determining its influence on processing.

Activation is a numeric value (0 to 100) assigned to each Slipnet node and Themespace theme, representing how relevant or active that concept currently is. Activation flows into the Slipnet from the Workspace when built structures use a concept, spreads along weighted links to neighboring nodes, and decays over time. Deeper concepts decay more slowly than shallow ones. A node with activation above 50% is considered fully active and can trigger top-down codelet posting. For example, if many successor bonds are built, the 'successor' node's activation rises, causing top-down scouts to look for more successor relationships.

### Bond

**Topic key:** `bond`  
**Summary:** A relationship between two adjacent objects in a string, characterized by a bond category and bond facet.

A bond represents a perceived relationship between two neighboring objects (letters or groups) within the same string. Each bond has a category (sameness, successor, or predecessor) and a facet (letter-category, length, alphabetic-position, or direction) that specifies along which dimension the relationship holds. For example, 'a' and 'b' can be connected by a successor bond on the letter-category facet, meaning 'b' is the successor of 'a' in the alphabet. Bonds are the building blocks of groups -- a sequence of same-category bonds in the same direction can be chunked into a group.

### Bond Category

**Topic key:** `bond_category`  
**Summary:** The type of relationship a bond represents: sameness, successor, or predecessor.

Bond category classifies the relationship that a bond represents between two adjacent objects. The three categories are: sameness (the two objects are identical along the bond facet, e.g., 'a' and 'a'), successor (the right/second object follows the left/first along the bond facet, e.g., 'a' followed by 'b'), and predecessor (the right/second object precedes the left/first, e.g., 'b' followed by 'a'). Sameness has high conceptual depth (around 80), making sameness-based structures more abstract and resistant to decay than successor or predecessor bonds (depth around 50).

### Bond Facet

**Topic key:** `bond_facet`  
**Summary:** The conceptual dimension along which a bond relationship is defined: letter-category, length, alphabetic-position, or direction.

A bond facet specifies which conceptual dimension a bond relationship is measured along. The four bond facets are: letter-category (the identity of letters, e.g., 'a' succeeded by 'b'), length (the size of groups, e.g., a group of 2 succeeded by a group of 3), alphabetic-position (the numeric position in the alphabet), and direction (the directionality of groups). When a bond-scout codelet examines two adjacent objects, it checks each facet for potential sameness, successor, or predecessor relationships. Letter-category bonds tend to have higher internal strength than length bonds.

### Bridge

**Topic key:** `bridge`  
**Summary:** A mapping between objects in different strings, carrying concept-mappings that describe how their descriptions correspond.

A bridge connects objects across two different strings, establishing a correspondence that is central to analogy-making. Each bridge carries one or more concept-mappings that describe how the descriptions of the two objects relate. Bridges come in three types: horizontal-top (between initial and modified strings), horizontal-bottom (between target and answer strings), and vertical (between initial and target, or between modified and answer). For example, a vertical bridge might map the 'c' in 'abc' to the 'z' in 'xyz', with a concept-mapping of 'rightmost -> rightmost' (identity) or 'leftmost -> rightmost' (slippage via opposite).

### Clamping

**Topic key:** `clamping`  
**Summary:** Holding a node, theme, or the temperature at a fixed activation level for a specified period to force focused exploration.

Clamping is the act of freezing a value -- whether a Slipnet node's activation, a Themespace theme's activation, or the global temperature -- at a fixed level for a defined number of codelet steps or update cycles. During initialization, relevant Slipnet nodes are clamped at full activation for a warm-up period to bootstrap the system. During snag response, temperature may be clamped at a moderately high value to prevent premature convergence. During jootsing, negative theme activation may be clamped to inhibit a stuck interpretation. Clamping overrides normal activation dynamics and is always temporary, expiring after a set duration.

### Codelet

**Topic key:** `codelet`  
**Summary:** A small, specialized agent that performs a single micro-task during one step of Metacat's processing.

Codelets are the fundamental units of computation in Metacat. Each codelet is a small procedure focused on one specific micro-task, such as looking for a potential bond between two adjacent letters, evaluating a proposed group's strength, or attempting to build a bridge between objects in different strings. Codelets are stored in the Coderack and selected probabilistically for execution. For example, a 'bond-scout' codelet might notice that letters 'a' and 'b' are adjacent and propose a successor bond between them. Over 40 distinct codelet types exist, organized into families: scouts, evaluators, builders, breakers, rule codelets, bridge codelets, progress-watchers, jootsers, and answer-justifiers.

### Concept Mapping

**Topic key:** `concept_mapping`  
**Summary:** A correspondence between two descriptions carried by a bridge, indicating identity, slippage, or coattail relationship.

A concept-mapping is a correspondence between a description on one object and a description on another, carried by a bridge that connects them across strings. Concept-mappings come in three types: identity (the same concept maps to itself, e.g., 'rightmost -> rightmost'), slippage (one concept maps to a related but different concept, e.g., 'leftmost -> rightmost' via the opposite relation), and coattail (a mapping that follows from other mappings rather than being independently motivated). Concept-mapping strength rewards conceptual depth while slippability penalizes it, meaning deep identity mappings are very strong and deep slippages are very hard to achieve.

### Conceptual Depth

**Topic key:** `conceptual_depth`  
**Summary:** An intrinsic property of each Slipnet node indicating how abstract or fundamental the concept is.

Conceptual depth is a fixed numeric property (0-100) of each Slipnet node that measures how abstract, fundamental, or 'deep' the concept is. Shallow concepts like individual letters have low conceptual depth (around 10) and are easy to activate but exert weak influence. Deep concepts like 'opposite' and 'identity' have high conceptual depth (around 90) -- they are harder to activate but, once active, resist decay more strongly and exert greater influence on processing. Conceptual depth also factors into rule quality: rules that use deeper concepts (like 'replace the rightmost letter by its successor') are rated as more abstract and higher-quality than rules using shallow concepts (like 'replace c by d').

### Description

**Topic key:** `description`  
**Summary:** A semantic property attached to a Workspace object, pairing a description type with a descriptor from the Slipnet.

A description is a semantic label attached to a Workspace object (letter or group) that identifies one of its properties. Each description pairs a description type (such as letter-category, string-position, or object-category) with a descriptor (the actual Slipnet concept, such as 'a', 'leftmost', or 'letter'). For example, the letter 'a' at the start of string 'abc' would have descriptions including (letter-category: a), (string-position: leftmost), and (object-category: letter). Descriptions are the basis for concept-mappings in bridges -- when a bridge connects two objects, it maps their descriptions to each other. A description is considered relevant when its type is fully active in the Slipnet.

### Deterministic Replay

**Topic key:** `deterministic_replay`  
**Summary:** The ability to reproduce an exact run by reusing the same random number seed, enabling debugging and analysis.

Deterministic replay is the ability to reproduce an exact sequence of processing steps by supplying the same random number seed that was used in a previous run. Because all of Metacat's stochastic decisions (codelet selection, structure-building fights, probabilistic choices) are driven by a pseudorandom number generator, providing the same seed guarantees identical behavior. This is invaluable for debugging, analysis, and demonstration -- the dissertation's sample runs all include their random seeds in an appendix so they can be reproduced exactly. In Petacat, deterministic replay support ensures that any run can be saved and re-examined step by step.

### Direction

**Topic key:** `direction`  
**Summary:** The orientation of a group or bond sequence within a string: left or right.

Direction indicates the orientation of a group or sequence of bonds within a string. A group with direction 'right' reads naturally from left to right (e.g., 'abc' as a rightward successor-group), while a group with direction 'left' reads from right to left (e.g., 'cba' as a leftward successor-group, since reading right-to-left yields c, b, a -- a successor sequence). Sameness bonds have no direction. Direction is one of the Slipnet concepts that commonly participates in slippage: 'left' and 'right' are connected by the 'opposite' link, so in problems involving reversal, direction slippage is a key mechanism for discovering the analogy.

### Group Category

**Topic key:** `group_category`  
**Summary:** The classification of a group based on the bond type shared by its members: same-group, successor-group, or predecessor-group.

A group category describes the type of internal regularity that defines a group. The three categories are: same-group (all internal bonds are sameness bonds, e.g., the group 'aaa'), successor-group (all internal bonds are successor bonds, e.g., the group 'abc'), and predecessor-group (all internal bonds are predecessor bonds, e.g., the group 'cba'). The group category, along with the bond facet and direction, fully characterizes the group's structure. For example, 'abc' is a successor-group on the letter-category facet with rightward direction.

### Jootsing

**Topic key:** `jootsing`  
**Summary:** "Jumping Out Of The System" -- a meta-strategy for detecting and escaping repetitive failure patterns.

Jootsing (an acronym for 'Jumping Out Of The System,' coined by Douglas Hofstadter) is Metacat's meta-level strategy for breaking out of unproductive processing loops. When jootser codelets detect that the system has encountered repeated snags with overlapping thematic patterns, they analyze the common themes of the failures and clamp negative activation on those themes, actively inhibiting the stuck interpretation. This forces the system to explore alternative perspectives. For example, if the system keeps failing while trying to map strings left-to-right, jootsing might inhibit the left-to-right theme, opening up the possibility of a right-to-left mapping. If all alternatives are exhausted, the system may give up on the problem.

### Proposal Level

**Topic key:** `proposal_level`  
**Summary:** The lifecycle stage of a Workspace structure: proposed, evaluated, or built.

Every structure in the Workspace passes through three proposal levels during its lifecycle. A structure begins as 'proposed' when a scout codelet first suggests it. It advances to 'evaluated' when an evaluator codelet assesses its strength. Finally, it reaches 'built' when a builder codelet successfully promotes it, at which point it becomes part of the active Workspace interpretation. Structures can fizzle at the evaluation stage if they are too weak, or be broken after building if a stronger competing structure displaces them. Only built structures contribute to temperature calculation, activate Slipnet concepts, and generate Themespace themes.

### Rule

**Topic key:** `rule`  
**Summary:** A transformation description that characterizes how one string changes into another, built from bridges and measured by quality.

A rule describes the transformation from one string to another, capturing both what changed and at what level of abstraction. Rules contain intrinsic clauses (changes to individual objects, such as 'replace the rightmost letter by its successor') and extrinsic clauses (group-level changes, such as 'increase the length of the rightmost group by one'). Rule quality is measured along three dimensions: uniformity (consistency of the concept-mappings), abstractness (how deep the concepts used are), and succinctness (how compactly the rule can be stated). Rules are the structures that get translated across strings to produce answers.

### Salience

**Topic key:** `salience`  
**Summary:** A measure of how attention-worthy an object is, combining its importance and unhappiness values.

Salience determines how likely a Workspace object is to be noticed and acted upon by scout codelets. It is computed as a combination of the object's importance (based on how many descriptions, bonds, and bridges it participates in) and its unhappiness (inversely related to how well it is integrated into the current interpretation). Intra-string salience weights unhappiness 80% and importance 20%, so lonely objects attract within-string attention. Inter-string salience inverts this (importance 80%, unhappiness 20%), so well-described objects attract cross-string mapping attention. An isolated letter with no bonds would have high unhappiness and thus high intra-string salience, making it a likely target for bottom-up scouts.

### Slippage

**Topic key:** `slippage`  
**Summary:** A conceptual substitution where one concept is fluidly replaced by a related concept during analogy-making.

Slippage is the mechanism by which Metacat achieves fluid, creative analogies rather than rigid structural mappings. When activation patterns in the Slipnet cause normally-distinct concepts to become interchangeable, a slippage occurs. For example, in the problem 'abc -> abd; xyz -> ?', the concept 'leftmost' may slip to 'rightmost' via the 'opposite' link, enabling the system to see 'z' (the rightmost letter) as analogous to 'c' (the rightmost letter) rather than 'x' (the leftmost). Slippages are carried by concept-mappings in bridges and are central to how Metacat produces answers like 'xyd' instead of the literal 'xya'.

### Snag

**Topic key:** `snag`  
**Summary:** A progress stall detected by a progress-watcher codelet when the system fails to build new significant structures.

A snag is a detected stall in Metacat's processing, identified by progress-watcher codelets when the system has been running for many steps without building any new significant structures or when a rule translation fails. When a snag is recorded, the system responds by clamping temperature to prevent premature convergence and posting focused codelets to explore alternative structures. Repeated snags with overlapping themes trigger the jootsing mechanism. For example, if the system repeatedly tries and fails to translate a rule because the target string lacks a required letter, a snag records this pattern so jootsing can inhibit the stuck interpretation.

### Spanning Group

**Topic key:** `spanning_group`  
**Summary:** A group that encompasses an entire string, treating the whole string as a single structured unit.

A spanning group is a group whose members collectively cover every position in a string, effectively chunking the entire string into one structured object. For example, in the string 'abc', if all three letters are bonded by successor bonds and grouped together, the result is a spanning group of type 'successor-group' with direction 'right'. Spanning groups always have 100% external strength and are significant because they enable the system to describe and manipulate entire strings as single units, leading to more abstract and powerful rules. They are also important for bridge-building, since a spanning group can be mapped to a spanning group in another string.

### Temperature (Glossary)

**Topic key:** `temperature_glossary`  
**Summary:** A global randomness parameter ranging from 0 (deterministic, confident) to 100 (chaotic, confused).

Temperature is a global value (0 to 100) that inversely measures how well the system has organized the problem. It is computed from the quantity and quality of built structures and updated every 15 codelets. High temperature (near 100) means little structure exists, so the system explores broadly with near-random codelet selection. Low temperature (near 0) means strong structures are in place, so selection becomes greedy and focused. Temperature regulates all stochastic decisions in the system, implementing the exploration-exploitation tradeoff.

### Theme

**Topic key:** `theme`  
**Summary:** An activated pattern along a conceptual dimension in the Themespace, characterizing an aspect of the current interpretation.

A theme represents an activated conceptual pattern within the Themespace, organized by bridge type and dimension. Themes capture the character of the interpretation being built -- for example, 'in the top bridge, the direction dimension shows a left-to-right identity mapping.' Each theme has positive and negative activation values, and themes within a cluster compete through excitatory and inhibitory interactions. When a theme becomes dominant in its cluster, it exerts thematic pressure that biases codelet posting and structure evaluation toward that interpretation.

### Update Cycle

**Topic key:** `update_cycle`  
**Summary:** A periodic maintenance step occurring every 15 codelet executions that recomputes strengths, activation, temperature, and posts new codelets.

An update cycle is a periodic maintenance step that occurs every 15 codelet executions. During an update cycle, the system: (a) recomputes all structure strengths in the Workspace, (b) updates object importance and salience values, (c) spreads activation from the Workspace to the Slipnet and Themespace, (d) spreads activation within the Slipnet and Themespace, (e) recomputes temperature, (f) posts new bottom-up and top-down codelets to the Coderack, and (g) updates graphical displays. The update cycle is the heartbeat of Metacat's control loop, synchronizing all components of the architecture.

### Urgency

**Topic key:** `urgency`  
**Summary:** A codelet's priority level (one of seven bins) that influences its probability of being selected from the Coderack.

Urgency is a discrete priority level assigned to each codelet when it is posted to the Coderack. The seven urgency bins are: extremely-low, very-low, low, medium, high, very-high, and extremely-high (with numeric values 7, 21, 35, 49, 63, 77, and 91 respectively). Higher urgency increases a codelet's probability of being selected, but the effect is modulated by temperature -- at high temperature, urgency matters little (selection is nearly random), while at low temperature, urgency strongly biases selection (high-urgency codelets dominate). Urgency is typically set based on the strength of the structure the codelet is meant to act on, or the activation of the Slipnet concept that triggered it.

### Workspace Structure

**Topic key:** `workspace_structure`  
**Summary:** Any perceptual entity built in the Workspace: bonds, groups, bridges, descriptions, or rules.

A workspace structure is the general term for any perceptual entity that exists in the Workspace. The six types are: letters (atomic elements), descriptions (semantic properties attached to objects), bonds (relationships between adjacent objects), groups (structured collections of objects), bridges (mappings across strings), and rules (transformation descriptions). All structures except letters share a common lifecycle (proposed -> evaluated -> built), have computed strength values combining internal coherence and external support, and can be broken by competing structures. The set of built structures at any moment defines the system's current interpretation of the problem.
